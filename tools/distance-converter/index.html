About these units

Metric (SI)

Based on the metre (m), the SI base unit of length. Multiples and submultiples use powers of 10: nanometre (nm), micrometre (µm), millimetre (mm), centimetre (cm), metre (m), kilometre (km). Used worldwide in science, engineering, and most countries for everyday distances. The metre was originally defined from the Earth’s circumference; today it’s defined from the speed of light.

Imperial and US customary

Inch (in), foot (ft), yard (yd), and mile (mi) are still used in the US, UK, and a few other places for everyday use (road signs, height, short distances). The nautical mile (nmi) is exactly 1,852 m by international agreement; it equals one minute of latitude and is used in aviation and shipping. US and UK “statute” miles are the same (1,609.344 m).

Astronomical scales

Astronomical unit (AU) — mean distance from Earth to the Sun (~149.6 million km). Used for distances within the Solar System (e.g. Mars ~1.5 AU).

Light-second (ls), light-minute (lm) — distance light travels in 1 second or 1 minute in a vacuum. Handy for inner Solar System (Earth–Moon ~1.3 ls, Earth–Sun ~8.3 lm).

Light-year (ly) — distance light travels in one Julian year (~9.46 trillion km). Used for stellar and galactic distances (e.g. nearest star ~4.2 ly).

Parsec (pc) — about 3.26 light-years. One parsec is the distance at which 1 AU subtends 1 arcsecond. Used in professional astronomy (e.g. “parallax in parsecs”).
